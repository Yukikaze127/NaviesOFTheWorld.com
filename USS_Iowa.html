USS Iowa BB-61

Introduction

The USS Iowa (BB-61), the lead ship of her class, is a lasting symbol of American naval power and technology. She was built at the New York Navy Yard in Brooklyn, New York. Her keel was laid down on June 27, 1940. She launched on August 27, 1942, and officially joined the US Navy on February 22, 1943. As the first of the Navy's new class of large and agile battleships for World War II, the Iowa-class vessel weighed 45,000 tons and measured an impressive 887 feet 3 inches in length. Her crew included 151 officers and 2,637 enlisted personnel. Commissioned during the peak of World War II, the USS Iowa quickly became a powerful force, earning 11 battle stars for her service. Her early responsibilities included transporting President Franklin D. Roosevelt across the Atlantic to the Tehran Conference in late 1943. The USS Iowa (BB-61) proudly carried the name of the great state of Iowa, serving in not only World War II but also in the Korean War and the Vietnam War. This history solidified her legacy as a versatile and strong warship across several decades of American history.

Full Breakdown

Chapter 1

Construction and Commissioning

USS Iowa (BB-61), the lead ship of her class, was born from America‚Äôs urgent naval expansion under the Second Vinson Act of 1939. Her keel was laid at the New York Naval Shipyard on 27 June 1940, where thousands of workers labored to shape her massive hull. On 27 August 1942, she slid into the East River, sponsored by Ilo Wallace, wife of Vice President Henry A. Wallace. The months that followed saw her fitted with cutting-edge firepower, armor, and radar systems that made her one of the fastest and most formidable battleships afloat. Finally, on 22 February 1943, under Captain John L. McCrea, she was commissioned into service, ready to project American power across the oceans in the darkest days of World War II..

Chapter 2

Design and Capabilities

USS Iowa was not merely a warship; she was a floating fortress built for speed and power. Her main battery of nine 16-inch/50 caliber Mark 7 guns, mounted in three turrets, gave her the ability to hurl shells weighing over a ton more than 20 miles, leveling fortifications or crippling enemy vessels with a single broadside. For defense, she carried 20 5-inch dual-purpose guns, able to strike both aircraft and surface targets. Beneath her armored decks lay eight boilers and four turbines, producing over 212,000 horsepower and driving her sleek hull to speeds above 33 knots‚Äîunheard of for battleships of her size. This blend of speed and strength earned her the nickname ‚Äúfast battleship,‚Äù allowing her to keep pace with aircraft carriers. Her armor, up to 12 inches thick at the belt, protected her vitals against enemy fire, while advanced radar and fire-control systems gave her unmatched accuracy. Housing nearly 2,800 officers and crew, Iowa was a self-sustaining city at sea, with workshops, galleys, and medical facilities to keep her fighting through long campaigns. Every aspect of her design reflected America‚Äôs determination to dominate the seas in World War II.

USS Iowa's service record is a testament to her design and the men who served aboard her. During World War II, she participated in numerous battles, including the Battle of the Philippine Sea and the Battle of Leyte Gulf, where her guns silenced enemy positions and protected American forces. After the war, she was decommissioned but remained a symbol of naval power.

USS Iowa's post-war career was as storied as her combat service. After being decommissioned in 1949, she was reactivated during the Korean War, where she provided naval gunfire support for ground troops. Her powerful guns were instrumental in the bombardment of enemy positions, showcasing her enduring relevance on the modern battlefield.

Chapter 3

WWII: Atltantic to Pacific

Iowa made a strong entrance early in World War II, starting her service in the Atlantic. In November 1943, she transported President Franklin D. Roosevelt across the Atlantic to North Africa on the way to the Tehran Conference. This significant task earned her the nickname ‚ÄúBattleship of Presidents.‚Äù

In early 1944, Iowa moved to the Pacific, playing a key role in the Marshalls campaign. She shelled beachheads at Kwajalein and Eniwetok. She also screened fast carrier groups, attacked Japanese bases at Truk, and provided fire support across the Marianas, including Saipan, Tinian, Guam, Peleliu, and Leyte. Her active participation during the Battle of the Philippine Sea and Leyte Gulf highlighted her importance in America‚Äôs island-hopping strategy.

After an overhaul in early 1945, Iowa returned for the Okinawa campaign and the raids on the Japanese home islands. She bombarded industrial targets in Hokkaido and Honshu. Finally, she sailed into Tokyo Bay as Admiral Halsey‚Äôs flagship for Japan‚Äôs formal surrender on 2 September 1945. This marked a symbolic end to her wartime service.

Post-War and the Korean Conflict

After returning to the U.S., Iowa supported post-war operations and training until she was first decommissioned in March 1949. The start of the Korean War brought her back. She was recommissioned in August 1951 and served as the Seventh Fleet flagship through 1952. During this time, she carried out heavy shore bombardments against enemy targets in North Korea, including Songjin and Hungnam.

After her Korean deployment, Iowa joined the Atlantic Fleet. She went on several training cruises in Europe and participated in NATO exercises. She was also present for the International Naval Review in Hampton Roads in 1957. She was decommissioned again in February 1958.

Cold War Revival: The 1980s Resurgence

After more than twenty years in the reserve fleet, Iowa was brought back to service during the Reagan-era push for a 600-ship Navy. She was recommissioned in April 1984 and updated with modern weapons, including Tomahawk and Harpoon missiles, Phalanx CIWS, improved radar, and electronics. This reaffirmed \her role as a forward-looking deterrent in a new age of naval warfare.

Iowa sailed through European waters in the mid-1980s. She entered the Mediterranean and later the Indian Ocean and Arabian Sea, representing American power and determination during increased East-West tensions.

Tragedy and Final Decommissioning

The resurgence came with tragedy. On 19 April 1989, during a gunnery exercise, an explosion inside her No. 2 turret killed 47 sailors. This was one of the Navy‚Äôs deadliest peacetime disasters. While the official Navy investigation blamed sabotage, many have debated this conclusion. Independent reviews have suggested other possible causes.

Despite the incident, Iowa continued with limited deployments but was decommissioned for the last time in October 1990. She remained in reserve until being removed from the Naval Vessel Register in 2006.

A Museum, A Memory

In 2011, the Pacific Battleship Center received the Iowa. By 2012, she had secured a permanent spot at Berth 87 in the Port of Los Angeles. After opening to the public, she became the Battleship USS Iowa Museum. This museum educates visitors and stands as an important piece of naval history. Today, she is the main attraction of the National Museum of the Surface Navy and a highlight of Los Angeles Fleet Week.

Visitors and veterans reflect on her legacy not just as a massive vessel of steel and firepower, but also as a symbol of service, sacrifice, and changing purpose.
